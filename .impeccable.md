# Design Context

## Users

A personal corner of the internet for Patrick Dewey — a backend engineer and atproto enthusiast. Visitors fall into three overlapping groups: (1) Patrick himself, using the site as a logbook; (2) friends and strangers who found their way in from Bluesky, GitHub, or project pages; (3) atproto / coffee / music niche visitors arriving via the `/coffee`, `/music`, and (soon) more atproto-integrated surfaces.

The site is not optimizing for a hiring funnel or lead generation. It is not a résumé. It is a personal research notebook that happens to be public.

Job-to-be-done: let a curious visitor dig around — read a post, see what Patrick's been brewing, see what's playing, follow the atproto trail — and come away with a genuine sense of what Patrick cares about.

## Brand Personality

Three words: **lived-in, mechanical, honest.**

The voice is that of a well-worn engineering notebook — precise where precision matters, loose where it doesn't, no marketing veneer. Patrick's writing is plain, slightly dry, and self-aware (see `/now`: "it would help if I wrote something more recently than last August"). The site should extend that voice, not contradict it.

Emotional goals: **quiet confidence, minor delight on small details, nothing performative.** No hero section. No "about me." The page is the content.

## Aesthetic Direction

**Terminal-first / hacker almanac.** Think: a well-maintained `.plan` file crossed with a printed technical manual. Dense rather than airy. Rewards scanning. Every pixel earns its place.

Concrete directions:
- Monospace as the system, not as decoration. Use real column alignment, box-drawing characters, ASCII dividers, and tables where they help — not as "look technical" sprinkles.
- Keep the earthy dark palette (`--background: #24211e`, warm cream primary, green/orange/teal/yellow/red semantic accents). This is already distinctive and right for the brand — refine it with OKLCH, but do not replace it.
- Break mono-monotony with a single printed-manual display face for headings. Iosevka Patrick (custom) stays as the body. Redaction (or similar weathered slab) for display.
- Keyboard-driven affordances where they make sense (e.g., `/`, `?`, `j/k` navigation on the blog index) — small, optional, not mandatory.
- Surface atproto identity where relevant. The `at://` URI already lives in post `<head>` tags; consider making it visible in the UI, not just metadata. Link to the PDS. Show the record rkey.

**Anti-references:**
- Generic SaaS landing page (cards, rounded corners, gradient buttons, "Built with Next.js" templates)
- Portfolio-site-as-resume (headshot hero, "About / Experience / Skills" sections, job timeline)
- Over-designed agency site (scroll-jacking, full-bleed video, awwwards.com energy)

## Design Principles

1. **The page is the manifest.** No hero, no intro paragraph, no "welcome." Land the visitor directly into content or into an index of content. Headers can be terse labels or even just a timestamp.

2. **Density is a feature, not a bug.** Pack the homepage with real signal — a recent play, a recent brew, the latest post, the `/now` headline — arranged as a dashboard, not a brochure. Treat every surface like a `cat ~/.plan` dump.

3. **Monospace alignment is structural, not decorative.** If you use a mono font, earn it: columns should actually align. Dates, rkeys, and revision IDs should sit in fixed-width slots. Otherwise pair mono with a proper display face and let the body breathe.

4. **Semantic color over decorative color.** The existing palette maps colors to heading levels (teal/orange/red/yellow) — lean into that as a system. Each accent color should mean something, not exist for variety.

5. **Small honest details beat big flashy gestures.** The jj revision in the footer is the right size of flex. Keep that energy. A terminal cursor blink on a label, a hand-tuned timestamp formatter, a `<time>` element that's actually screen-reader-friendly — these beat any animation.
